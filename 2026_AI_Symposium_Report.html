Responses collected June 9–18, 2026  ·  50 respondents  ·  Anonymous survey

1Overview

This report summarizes feedback from the post-event survey for the 2026 UCLA Teaching Symposium (Adapting Instruction in the Age of AI). It covers four areas — the overall experience, the keynote and faculty panel, the concurrent sessions, and the Tech Expo and Social Hour — along with open-ended comments, and is intended to inform future AI-related teaching and learning programming. The headline figures are below.

Strengths

Practical, applicable content. (3 comments) Respondents valued exposure to new tools and instructional approaches that they could apply directly in their teaching.

#3"What I also deeply appreciated about the event was that it brought together faculty interested in teaching. At a research university that's often a secondary focus. … I felt uplifted by the day."
#5"The lunchtable conversation (not just Tao) was the best part of the whole symposium."
#18"I was able to connect with other people from different departments."

Keynote and panel. (2 comments) These sessions were repeatedly praised as excellent and intellectually substantive, including by those who attended remotely.

#22"I participated via zoom for the keynote and the panel. Each of these sessions were excellent for different reasons. … These were compelling and thoughtful."
#6"The zoom-available panel was very good, full of insights and actionable ideas for the classroom, and assignments, and assessments."

Event organization. (2 comments) Respondents commended the quality of the organization and the professionalism and warmth of the TLC staff.

#1"The TLC staff was exceptionally welcoming, professional and supportive. I thought the event was remarkably well organized."
#16"This symposium was very organized. Thank you for inviting knowledgeable, innovative, and a diverse panel of speakers."

Emphasis on faculty expertise. (2 comments) Several respondents welcomed the event's grounding in faculty voices and expressed hope that the TLC would offer similar programming in the future.

#12"I appreciated that this event drew on faculty expertise and faculty voices. Really helpful insights were shared. I hope that the TLC will view this as model a success and do more of it in the future."

Suggestions and Areas for Improvement

Tech Expo logistics. (2 comments) Respondents offered suggestions on signage, timing, follow-up, and the social framing of the Expo.

#18"The Tech Expo felt too short to visit all the stations meaningfully. It would be very helpful to receive a list of stations in advance … While QR codes were provided for follow-up, I'd prefer to have my questions answered on the spot at the expo itself, rather than being redirected to external websites."
#12"The booths were not sufficiently signposted / set off from the general mingling space. The presence of alcohol also made it feel more 'social' than 'tech'."

Panel format. (2 comments) Several respondents expressed a desire for more direct discussion and debate among panelists, as well as additional time for audience questions.

#19"For panel, I would have like to hear the panelists have a discussion and debate. The ask question/answer misses out on dynamic exchanges which often lead to new insights and perspectives."
#30"The two panels would have benefitted from a little more time for audience Q&A."

Balance of perspectives. (2 comments) Respondents suggested including critical or non-adopting voices alongside enthusiastic adopters.

#27"I wish that the first panel included critical non-users of AI. It felt very pro-AI. Also, the panel raised many issues with the application of AI in higher education."
#14"I think someone should have made the point that we have to teach students to be experts in something before the students can meaningfully engage a GenAI."

Remote access and resources. (2 comments) A number of respondents participated only via Zoom; some wished for an in-person option, and several requested that session summaries and related resources be shared afterward.

#20"I was only invited to the online Zoom kickoff which I very much enjoyed. I would have liked to have the option to attend in person."
#22"Will you all be sharing out summaries of these sessions and/or related resources?"

Future programming. (3 comments) Suggestions included hands-on sessions, a focus on assessment, inclusion of the student perspective, and follow-up events.

#23"It would be lovely if the second session (or a second day) was a practical course on syllabus writing/lesson plans and demonstrations of tools in the classroom."
#30"It also would have been interesting to have one or two undergraduate students offer their candid perspectives on how they and their classmates are using AI in their studies."
#24"I think a future event could include sessions on helping students avoid generative AI and chat bots …"

6Key Takeaways

  1. The symposium was very well received overall. Ratings for relevance (mean 4.64) and applicability (mean 4.28) were particularly strong, with 98% of respondents agreeing that the content was relevant to their professional work.
  2. The respondent pool was predominantly faculty (64%) and broadly cross-campus, spanning 14 divisions and approximately 28 departments — evidence of wide institutional interest.
  3. Practical, applied sessions outperformed broader discussion sessions, indicating a clear appetite for concrete, classroom-ready strategies.
  4. Tech Expo and Social Hour results reflect a small, likely in-person sub-sample (n ≈ 23–24 of 50): as an in-person-only offering, remote attendees could not participate and, we assume, typically left this question blank (see Section 1).
  5. There is demonstrated demand for follow-up programming, including hands-on workshops, shared resources, a wider range of perspectives, and greater inclusion of the student voice.

7Methodology and Limitations

The analysis is based on 50 survey responses, of which 49 were complete. Ratings for the keynote and panel, the concurrent sessions, and the Tech Expo and Social Hour reflect smaller sub-samples (n = 2–24), as not all respondents attended every component. Means derived from fewer than 10 respondents — flagged in the corresponding charts and tables — should be interpreted with particular caution.

Response rate. The reconciled attendee count was 325 (176 in-person and 149 via Zoom). Excluding TLC staff — 30 in-person and 3 on Zoom (33 total) — leaves 292 eligible attendees, yielding a response rate of approximately 17% (50 ÷ 292). Presenters and expo partners (44 in-person) are retained in this figure; if they were also excluded, the eligible base would be smaller and the rate correspondingly higher. Including TLC staff, the rate would be approximately 15% (50 ÷ 325).

The most significant limitation concerns participation mode. The survey did not ask whether respondents attended in person or remotely, and remote participation was not anticipated during the survey's design. Because remote and partial-day attendees experience the symposium differently from full-day, in-person attendees — and cannot participate in certain components at all — the combined results may present a skewed or biased picture of the overall experience. Future iterations of the survey should include an explicit question on participation mode, and ideally on the portion of the event attended, to enable accurate segmentation and comparison of results.
